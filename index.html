Allegation: Forced to Sign the Contract

High Severity Strong Defence

Client's Claim:

The client alleges he was coerced/forced into signing the construction agreement.

Our Defence (11 Points):

Full defence is covered in Section 5 above. Key highlights:

1. Client requested the draft agreement himself - twice on 17 Mar 2025
2. Took 7 days to review, returned with annotated counter-proposal
3. Client pressured contractor to finalize: "I would like to finalise the deal and move forward latest by tomorrow"
4. Client uploaded the final contract himself on 22 May 2025
5. Invoked contract terms for his own benefit 5+ times after signing
6. Paid Rs. 34.5L voluntarily over 8+ months - ratification by conduct
7. Client's father (M. Vasanthan) signed as WITNESS on the contract (Page 12). A witness cannot later claim the signatory was coerced — the witness role confirms the signing was voluntary and observed.
8. Client signed every page — all 12 pages bear the client's signature ("V. Perinbanadhan"), demonstrating page-by-page acknowledgment of every clause.
9. Contract explicitly states: "This agreement is irrevocable" (Page 12) — the client agreed to this term with full knowledge.
Risk Assessment: Very low risk. The chat evidence overwhelmingly disproves this allegation. The father's signature as witness, client's signature on all 12 pages, and the "irrevocable" clause make the coercion claim untenable. The doctrine of approbation and reprobation applies.

Allegation: MCA & RoC Legal Threat

High Severity Strong Defence

Client's Exact Words:

26 March 2026 (Line 796)
Client:
"Dear Ms. Krithika, To give you the benefit of doubt, my mother has sent out a formal notification about the complaint that would be raised with MCA & RoC. The emails have been sent across to the email addresses: Harish@speckle.in; harish@waavie.com; & info@upscape.build. Since she will not be able to take back the complaint with MCA and RoC, she has sent out this notification as a gesture, giving you a reasonable time to react."

Our Defence:

MCA/RoC jurisdiction: MCA (Ministry of Corporate Affairs) and RoC (Registrar of Companies) deal with corporate governance, not construction contract disputes. This appears to be a misdirected complaint.
Intimidation tactic: The threat came after a 3-month silence (last message was 8 Jan 2026). This appears to be a pressure tactic rather than a genuine legal remedy.
No prior legal notice: No formal legal notice under Section 80 CPC was served. A WhatsApp message is not a substitute for proper legal procedure.
Client's mother sent it: The notification was sent by the client's mother, not the client himself or a legal representative, which weakens its formal standing.
Notification, not complaint: The client himself calls it a "notification" and a "gesture" - not a formal complaint. It appears to be a last-resort pressure tactic.
Risk Assessment: Low risk in terms of legal merit. MCA/RoC complaints typically relate to company fraud, director misconduct, or non-compliance with Companies Act - not construction contract disputes. The appropriate forum would be a Consumer Forum or Civil Court. The contractor should consult with counsel on a pre-emptive response.

Section 7

Applicable Legal Principles

1. Free Consent - Indian Contract Act, Section 14

Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by coercion (Section 15), undue influence (Section 16), fraud (Section 17), misrepresentation (Section 18), or mistake (Section 20-22). The documentary evidence demonstrates the client's consent was entirely free, voluntary, and informed.

2. Doctrine of Approbation and Reprobation

A party cannot take advantage of a contract (invoking penalty clauses, material specifications, payment milestones, etc.) and simultaneously claim the contract was executed under coercion. The client's repeated reliance on the contract's terms to enforce his rights estops him from challenging the contract's validity.

3. Ratification by Conduct

Even if there were any initial reservations (which there were not), the client's conduct over 8+ months - making payments totalling Rs. 34.5 lakhs, participating actively in construction decisions, selecting premium materials, and enforcing contract terms - constitutes ratification of the agreement and waiver of any right to challenge its validity.

4. Burden of Proof - Indian Contract Act, Section 16(3)

The burden of proving coercion or undue influence lies on the party alleging it. The documentary evidence overwhelmingly contradicts any such claim and the client has produced no evidence to support his allegation.

Under the well-established legal doctrine of approbation and reprobation, a party who has accepted and derived benefits from a contract is estopped from subsequently challenging its validity. The burden of proving coercion under Section 16(3) of the Indian Contract Act rests squarely on the party alleging it, and in this case, the available evidence not only fails to support the allegation but affirmatively disproves it.
