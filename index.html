Section 7

Applicable Legal Principles

1. Free Consent - Indian Contract Act, Section 14

Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by coercion (Section 15), undue influence (Section 16), fraud (Section 17), misrepresentation (Section 18), or mistake (Section 20-22). The documentary evidence demonstrates the client's consent was entirely free, voluntary, and informed.

2. Doctrine of Approbation and Reprobation

A party cannot take advantage of a contract (invoking penalty clauses, material specifications, payment milestones, etc.) and simultaneously claim the contract was executed under coercion. The client's repeated reliance on the contract's terms to enforce his rights estops him from challenging the contract's validity.

3. Ratification by Conduct

Even if there were any initial reservations (which there were not), the client's conduct over 8+ months - making payments totalling Rs. 34.5 lakhs, participating actively in construction decisions, selecting premium materials, and enforcing contract terms - constitutes ratification of the agreement and waiver of any right to challenge its validity.

4. Burden of Proof - Indian Contract Act, Section 16(3)

The burden of proving coercion or undue influence lies on the party alleging it. The documentary evidence overwhelmingly contradicts any such claim and the client has produced no evidence to support his allegation.

Section 8

Summary of Evidence

Detailed Summary

The allegation of coercion raised by Mr. Perinbanathan Vasanthan is entirely without merit and is comprehensively contradicted by the documentary evidence preserved in the WhatsApp group chat "Mr Peri_Villa Hosur", spanning 796 messages from 15 March 2025 to 26 March 2026. The evidence unequivocally establishes that it was the client himself who initiated the contract process by requesting the draft agreement on 17 March 2025 - not once, but twice on the same evening. The contractor, Ms. Krithika, shared the draft the following day with expressly invitational language: "pls have a look and let me know if you need to add or remove anything." The client then took seven full days to independently review the document, during which time he consulted with his parents, prepared a detailed clause-by-clause annotated PDF, proposed his own counter-terms, and adjusted the total contract value himself to Rs. 47,52,000 based on his own calculation of the built-up area. On 27 March 2025, it was the client - not the contractor - who imposed deadlines, stating he wished to "finalise the deal and move forward latest by tomorrow" and voluntarily offered to transfer the advance payment by asking for the contractor's account number. The contractor, far from exerting pressure, was in fact travelling and had to request time to respond, to which the client graciously replied: "If you need a bit more time please feel free to ask for. Take care."

The negotiation process spanned approximately two months (17 March to 22 May 2025), involving multiple rounds of draft exchange, in-person meetings at the client's residence (where his parents were present), and telephonic discussions. The final executed contract was uploaded to the WhatsApp group by the client himself on 22 May 2025, titled "Final Contract AVS 423.pdf" - an act that demonstrates clear ownership, acceptance, and endorsement of the agreement. Following execution, the client proceeded to make voluntary payments totalling Rs. 34,50,000/- across Stages 1, 2, and partial Stage 3, attended the ground-breaking pooja ceremony on 23 April 2025, made numerous site visits, installed a borewell submersible pump at his own expense, arranged for the TNEB electrical connection, selected premium APCO blocks at additional cost, and added his father to the WhatsApp group for active family participation in the project.

Most critically, the client invoked the contract's provisions for his own benefit on at least five separate occasions between September and November 2025 - citing the waterproofing clause, the penalty clause for late delivery, the material specification for A-grade wood, and the payment milestone structure. His father, Mr. Vasanthan Munusamy, independently sought a signed copy of the agreement on 22 November 2025, requesting "the original agreement duly signed with sketches & 2d and programme schedule" - treating it as a valid and binding document. Under the well-established legal doctrine of approbation and reprobation, a party who has accepted and derived benefits from a contract is estopped from subsequently challenging its validity. The client's conduct over 8+ months - making substantial payments, actively participating in construction decisions, enforcing contractual terms, and never once raising the issue of coercion until the present dispute over pending payments arose - constitutes unambiguous ratification by conduct. Furthermore, the client is an educated professional employed at HAL (Hindustan Aeronautics Limited), a government defence organisation, possessing demonstrated technical knowledge of structural engineering, building approval processes, and legal terminology. He is not a person who could be characterised as vulnerable to coercion or lacking the capacity to comprehend the document he voluntarily negotiated, modified, signed, distributed, paid upon, and enforced for over eight months.

In light of the above, the allegation of coercion appears to be an afterthought raised in the context of a payment dispute, rather than a genuine grievance. The entire body of evidence - comprising the client's own messages, his annotated documents, his voluntary payments, his enforcement of contract clauses, and his family's acceptance of the agreement - conclusively demonstrates that the contract was entered into with free consent, full awareness, and active participation of the client. The burden of proving coercion under Section 16(3) of the Indian Contract Act rests squarely on the party alleging it, and in this case, the available evidence not only fails to support the allegation but affirmatively disproves it.
